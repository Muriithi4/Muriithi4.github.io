About

I am a bioinformatician with a strong foundation in molecular biology and computational analysis, with research interests centered on genomic surveillance, antimalarial drug resistance, and population genetics of malaria parasites and vectors.

My work focuses on the analysis of large-scale genomic datasets to identify and interpret genetic variation relevant to infectious disease transmission and control. I have experience working with public and field-derived datasets, applying statistical analysis, variant profiling, and data visualization to extract biologically meaningful insights.

I am particularly interested in integrating genomics with epidemiological and evolutionary frameworks to support malaria surveillance and evidence- based decision-making. My long-term goal is to pursue doctoral training in computational and evolutionary genomics with an emphasis on infectious diseases of global health importance.

Academic Background

I hold a Master of Science in Molecular Biology and Bioinformatics from Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology (JKUAT). My academic work has focused on the identification and functional characterisation of resistance-associated mutations in malaria parasites, supported by both wet-lab experimentation and computational analysis.

Projects

Structural and Functional Implications of MIT2 and NT2 Mutations in Amodiaquine and Piperaquine Resistant Plasmodium berghei

Journal Article — Experimental Parasitology

View Publication

This study investigated the molecular mechanisms underlying resistance to the antimalarial drugs amodiaquine and piperaquine in Plasmodium berghei, focusing on mutations in the transporter genes MIT2 and NT2. The project combined genomic analysis with structural modelling to evaluate how these mutations influence transporter protein function, parasite fitness, and drug susceptibility.

Structural and Functional Impact of the G340S Mutation in Plasmodium falciparumFerredoxin NADP⁺ Reductase: In silico Analysis and Molecular Docking

Preprint — VeriXiv

View Preprint

This project examined the structural and functional impact of the G340S mutation in PfFNR, an apicoplast enzyme involved in key biosynthetic pathways and antimalarial drug activation. Structural models were generated using AlphaFold and I-TASSER, followed by docking analyses with AutoDock Vina and molecular dynamics simulations using GROMACS.
