About

I am a bioinformatician with a strong foundation in molecular biology and computational analysis, with research interests centered on genomic surveillance, antimalarial drug resistance, and population genetics of malaria parasites and vectors.

My work focuses on the analysis of large-scale genomic datasets to identify and interpret genetic variation relevant to infectious disease transmission and control. I have experience working with public and field-derived datasets, applying statistical analysis, variant profiling, and data visualization to extract biologically meaningful insights.

I am particularly interested in integrating genomics with epidemiological and evolutionary frameworks to support malaria surveillance and evidence- based decision-making. My long-term goal is to pursue doctoral training in computational and evolutionary genomics, with an emphasis on infectious diseases of global health importance.

Projects

Structural and Functional Implications of MIT2 and NT2 Mutations in Amodiaquine and Piperaquine Resistant Plasmodium berghei

Journal article: Experimental Parasitology | https://doi.org/10.1016/j.exppara.2025.108923

This study investigated the molecular mechanisms underlying resistance to the antimalarial drugs amodiaquine and piperaquine in Plasmodium berghei, with a particular focus on mutations arising in membrane-associated transporter genes MIT2 and NT2. The project sought to determine how these mutations influence parasite fitness, drug susceptibility, and transporter protein function.

An integrative experimental and computational framework was employed. Genetically modified parasite lines with well-characterized resistance phenotypes were analysed alongside genomic and sequence-level data to identify resistance-associated mutations. Structural modelling and functional interpretation were then used to assess the impact of these mutations on transporter conformation, stability, and substrate interactions.

By directly linking specific genetic alterations to measurable resistance phenotypes, this work provides mechanistic insight into how transporter mutations mediate reduced antimalarial drug efficacy. The findings support molecular surveillance efforts for antimalarial resistance and contribute to the rational design and deployment of effective therapeutic strategies.

Structural and Functional Impact of the G340S Mutation in Plasmodium falciparum Ferredoxin NADP⁺ Reductase: In silico Analysis and Molecular Docking

Preprint: VeriXiv | https://doi.org/10.12688/verixiv.2019.1

In the context of identifying resistance markers associated with lumefantrine, preliminary experimental work identified a mutation in Plasmodium berghei Ferredoxin NADP⁺ Reductase (FNR), G332S, which corresponds to the G340S substitution in Plasmodium falciparum. PfFNR is an essential apicoplast-localized enzyme involved in critical biosynthetic pathways and antimalarial drug activation.

To characterise the structural and functional consequences of the G340S mutation, this project employed a comprehensive in silico bioinformatics pipeline. High-confidence wild-type and mutant PfFNR structural models were generated using AlphaFold3 and I-TASSER. Molecular docking analyses were performed with AutoDock Vina and AlphaFold3, followed by molecular dynamics simulations conducted in GROMACS. Structural visualisation and interaction mapping were carried out using ChimeraX.

Structural analyses revealed that the G340S substitution alters the conformation of the NADP⁺-binding loop, disrupts key hydrogen-bonding networks within the cofactor-overlapping region, and reduces predicted cofactor-binding affinity, while preserving the overall protein fold. These findings provide molecular-level insight into how single-point mutations in PfFNR may contribute to altered drug response phenotypes.
